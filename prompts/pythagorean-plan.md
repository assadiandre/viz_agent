1. TitleAndIntro (~15s)
Concept: Identifying a right triangle and its three sides.
Visual elements:
  - Title text "The Pythagorean Theorem": Top center.
  - Right-angled triangle: Centered.
  - Square right-angle indicator: Bottom corner between the two shorter sides.
  - Labels "Leg a" and "Leg b": Positioned next to the horizontal and vertical sides.
  - Label "Hypotenuse c": Positioned next to the longest, diagonal side.
Narrative sequence:
  1. The title appears at the top to establish the topic.
  2. A right-angled triangle is drawn in the center of the screen.
  3. The right-angle indicator pops in to emphasize the 90-degree corner. Text momentarily highlights: "Must be a right triangle!"
  4. The two shorter sides forming the right angle are labeled "Leg a" and "Leg b".
  5. The longest side is drawn with a slightly thicker line, and the label "Hypotenuse c" appears.
Key transitions: Draw the legs first, show the 90-degree angle, and then draw the hypotenuse connecting them, visually demonstrating that the hypotenuse is dictated by the legs.
Pacing notes: Pause after labeling the hypotenuse so the viewer can associate the names "leg" and "hypotenuse" with the letters a, b, and c.

2. TheGeometricMeaning (~30s)
Concept: The theorem literally refers to the area of geometric squares attached to the triangle's sides.
Visual elements:
  - The same right-angled triangle from Scene 1: Shifted slightly to the center-left.
  - Three geometric squares: One attached to each side of the triangle.
  - Text inside the squares: "$a^2$", "$b^2$", and "$c^2$".
  - The formula "$a^2 + b^2 = c^2$": Appears on the right side of the screen.
Narrative sequence:
  1. Starting with the bare triangle, a geometric square grows outward from side "a". The text "$a^2$" appears inside it, establishing that "a squared" means the area of an actual square.
  2. A second square grows outward from side "b" with the text "$b^2$" inside.
  3. A third, larger square grows outward from the hypotenuse "c", with the text "$c^2$".
  4. The equation "$a^2 + b^2 = c^2$" writes out on the right side.
  5. The squares for $a^2$ and $b^2$ pulse, followed by the square for $c^2$ pulsing, visually linking the shapes to the equation.
Key transitions: The text "$a^2$" should slide from the square on the diagram directly into the equation on the right, reinforcing that the formula is an equation of areas.
Pacing notes: Hold for 4-5 seconds after the full equation is formed so the viewer can look back and forth between the geometry and the algebra.

3. VisualProof (~45s)
Concept: A geometric proof showing *why* the areas $a^2$ and $b^2$ perfectly add up to $c^2$.
Visual elements:
  - Four identical right triangles: Each with sides a, b, c. Color-coded (e.g., solid fill).
  - A large outline box: A square of side length (a+b) that acts as a container.
  - Empty negative space: The uncolored areas inside the container.
  - Area labels: Text floating in the negative space.
Narrative sequence:
  1. Clear the previous screen. Bring in four identical right triangles.
  2. Arrange them inside the large outline box so their hypotenuses face inward, forming a tilted empty square in the dead center.
  3. Label this central empty square "$c^2$". Establish the idea: The total empty space inside this box is exactly $c^2$.
  4. Without changing the size of the outer box, slide the four triangles around. Group them into two rectangles in the corners of the box.
  5. The negative space is now split into two separate squares: a smaller one and a medium one.
  6. Label the smaller empty square "$a^2$" and the medium one "$b^2$".
  7. Text appears below: "Same box. Same 4 triangles. Empty space must be equal."
  8. Therefore, $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$ appears at the bottom.
Key transitions: The sliding of the four triangles is the critical "Aha!" moment. The transition must be a smooth, continuous animation so the viewer sees no area is added or removed, just rearranged.
Pacing notes: Give the viewer a solid 5 seconds to stare at the newly arranged shapes to realize the two new empty squares correspond perfectly to the legs "a" and "b".

4. TheClassicExample (~35s)
Concept: Applying the formula to real numbers to find a missing side.
Visual elements:
  - Right triangle: Centered, but specifically drawn to a 3:4:5 proportion.
  - A grid background: Faint lines to show units of measurement.
  - Labels: "a = 3", "b = 4", "c = ?".
  - Step-by-step equation: Moving from $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$ to the final answer.
Narrative sequence:
  1. The screen clears and a faint grid appears. A right triangle is drawn on the grid.
  2. The horizontal leg spans 3 grid units. Label it "a = 3".
  3. The vertical leg spans 4 grid units. Label it "b = 4".
  4. The hypotenuse is drawn. Label it "c = ?".
  5. The core formula $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$ appears on the right.
  6. The variables 'a' and 'b' are replaced by 3 and 4, showing $3^2 + 4^2 = c^2$.
  7. The math resolves step-by-step downwards: $9 + 16 = c^2$, then $25 = c^2$.
  8. Finally, a square root symbol applies to both sides, resolving to $c = 5$.
  9. The "?" on the diagram is crossed out and replaced with "5".
Key transitions: When substituting numbers into the formula, visually lift the "3" and "4" from the diagram and drop them directly into the equation to replace 'a' and 'b'.
Pacing notes: Ensure each line of the algebraic steps ($9+16$, $25$, $\sqrt{25}$) takes about 2 seconds so the mental math is easy to follow.

5. SummaryScene (~15s)
Concept: Reinforcing the core takeaway rule and its main condition.
Visual elements:
  - Universal formula "$a^2 + b^2 = c^2$": Large and centered.
  - Generic right triangle: Positioned above the formula.
  - Warning text: "Remember: ONLY for right triangles!" at the bottom of the screen.
Narrative sequence:
  1. The grid and math from the previous scene fade away.
  2. The formula shifts to the center and scales up to become the focal point.
  3. The right triangle sits above it. The right-angle square indicator pulses vividly in red.
  4. The warning text appears at the bottom, pointing out that this beautiful rule breaks if the 90-degree angle changes.
Key transitions: The pulsing of the right-angle square perfectly syncs with the appearance of the warning text to link cause and effect.
Pacing notes: Hold this final screen for 5 seconds to let the core rule burn into the viewer's memory.
